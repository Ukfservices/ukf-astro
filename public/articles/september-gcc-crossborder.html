GCC Cross-Border Shipping Guide 2026

By UKF Services  ·  7 min read  ·  Updated September 2026

Cross-border road freight is the backbone of intra-GCC trade. The UAE sits at the centre of the region and acts as a distribution hub for Saudi Arabia, Oman, Kuwait, Bahrain, and Qatar. If you are moving goods by road across any of these borders, this guide covers what you need to know.

GCC Customs Union: The six GCC states form a customs union with a unified external tariff. Goods with a valid UAE Certificate of Origin — confirming UAE manufacture — move between GCC states under preferential duty treatment. If your goods are re-exported (originally from outside the GCC), different rules apply.

Saudi Arabia — what makes it different

Saudi Arabia is the most complex GCC destination for cross-border road freight. The volume of trade is high, the border can be congested, and the Saudi Authority for Industrial Cities and Technology Zones (MODON) and SFDA both operate pre-clearance requirements for specific product categories.

Saber is Saudi Arabia's product conformity platform. For regulated products — which includes most electronics, appliances, toys, and food contact materials — the product must be registered on Saber before shipment. Without a Saber certificate, the goods will be held at the border. Registration takes days to weeks depending on the product category, so it must be arranged well ahead of the shipping date.

SFDA (Saudi Food and Drug Authority) controls the import of food, cosmetics, medical devices, and pharmaceuticals. Pre-approval or registration is required. If you are new to exporting these categories to Saudi Arabia, allow significant lead time for approvals.

Oman — the straightforward lane

UAE to Oman is one of the most efficient cross-border lanes in the region. The Hatta crossing (for Dubai origin) handles a high volume of daily trucks with relatively short dwell times. Dubai to Muscat can realistically be delivered in a single working day for morning departures.

Oman customs operates through the BAYAN system. Your UAE forwarder's Oman-side agent will handle the import declaration. Make sure the agent is confirmed before the cargo departs — delays at the Oman side typically come from the destination agent being unprepared, not from customs itself.

Common problems and how to avoid them

Incorrect HS codes. Each GCC country applies its own tariff schedule to the common external tariff. An HS code that results in zero duty in UAE may attract a different rate in Saudi Arabia. Verify destination-country duty rates before shipping — surprises at the border delay clearance and add cost.

Missing Saber certificate for Saudi Arabia. This is the most common cause of Saudi border holds for exporters new to the lane. If your product is in a regulated category, start the Saber registration process at least 2–3 weeks before your planned ship date.

Driver documentation. Cross-border truck drivers need valid visas for each country they enter. A driver without the correct visa for a transit country (e.g., Saudi for a Kuwait delivery) will be turned back at the border. This is a logistics coordination issue, not a paperwork issue — your forwarder should manage it, but it is worth confirming.

Weight limits. Saudi Arabia and Oman have axle weight limits that differ from UAE standards. Overloaded trucks will be stopped at the border. Confirm vehicle configuration and loading weight with your carrier before dispatch.
